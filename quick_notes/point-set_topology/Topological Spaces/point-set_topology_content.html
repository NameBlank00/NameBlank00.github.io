Topological Spaces

Definition. A topology on a set \( X \) is a collection \( \mathscr{T} \) of subsets \( X \) satisfying the following properties:
  1. The empty set \( \varnothing \) and the set \( X \) are in \( \mathscr{T} \).
  2. The union of any collection in \( \mathscr{T} \) are in \( \mathscr{T} \).
  3. The intersection of any finite collections in \( \mathscr{T} \) are in \( \mathscr{T} \).
A set \( X \) for which a topology \( \mathscr{T} \) is specified is called a topological space, denoted by the pair \( (X,\mathscr{T}) \). Often the specific mention of \( \mathscr{T} \) is omitted if no confusion arises.

If \( X \) is a topological space with topology \( \mathscr{T} \), and let \( U \) be a subset of \( X \). We say that \( U \) is an open set of \( X \) if \( U \) belongs to the collection \( \mathscr{T} \). From this terminology, the sets \( \varnothing \) and \( X \) are both open, and any arbitrary unions and finite intersections of open sets are open.

EXAMPLE. Let \( X \) be a set, then the collection of all subsets of \( X \) is a topology on \( X \), called the discrete topology. The collection consisting of only \( \varnothing \) and \( X \) is a topology on \( X \), called the trivial topology.

EXAMPLE. Let \( X \) be a set. Let \( \mathscr{T}_f \) be the collection of all subsets \( U \) of \( X \) such that \( X \! \setminus \! U \) is either finite or all of \( X \): $$ \mathscr{T}_f = \{ U \subset X \, | \, U = \varnothing \wedge X \! \setminus \! U \text{ is finite } \} $$
